## What the U.S. State Department Says

The U.S. State Department currently rates Egypt at **Level 2: Exercise Increased Caution**. This is the same rating as France, Germany, Spain, and many other popular travel destinations.

The specific concerns cited are primarily related to terrorism risk in the Sinai Peninsula (particularly near the Egyptian-Israeli border) and in border regions far from tourist areas.

**Major tourist destinations — Cairo, Luxor, Aswan, Hurghada, the Red Sea coast — are not in elevated-risk zones.** The pyramids, the Valley of the Kings, Karnak Temple, the Nile, the Egyptian Museum: these are all in areas where tourism is the primary economic activity and where the government maintains a visible security presence.

For the most current advisory, check: [travel.state.gov](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/international-travel/International-Travel-Country-Information-Pages/Egypt.html)

## Where American Tourists Actually Go — And What It's Like

The Egypt most American tourists visit is concentrated in a few areas:

**Cairo and Giza** — Home to the pyramids, the Egyptian Museum, the Khan el-Khalili bazaar, and Islamic Cairo. Heavily touristed, well-staffed with tourism police, and safe for visitors.

**Luxor** — The world's greatest open-air museum. Karnak Temple, the Valley of the Kings, Hatshepsut's Temple. The East and West Banks of Luxor are very safe. I take clients here multiple times a week.

**Aswan** — Quieter than Cairo or Luxor, known for its Nubian culture and the temples of Abu Simbel. Very relaxed, very safe.

**Hurghada and the Red Sea coast** — Resort town with an international airport. Heavily developed for tourism, large expat and tourist community.

**The Nile between Luxor and Aswan** — Cruise route, all private boat or felucca. Safe and peaceful.

None of these are conflict zones. The Sinai Peninsula, where the State Department has more specific concerns, is a completely different geographic area from where standard Egypt tours operate.

## Common Safety Questions from American Travelers

### Is it safe to walk around on my own?

In Luxor, Aswan, and Hurghada — yes, for the most part. The souks and main tourist streets are active and manageable for independent walkers.

Cairo is a megacity of 20+ million people. Like any major city, you benefit from knowing where you're going. I recommend exploring Cairo's main tourist areas (Islamic Cairo, Zamalek, Downtown) with your guide or at least a clear plan. Petty opportunism toward tourists can happen in heavily trafficked areas, as in any major tourist city worldwide.

### Is it safe for solo female travelers?

Harassment exists in Egypt, as it does in many countries in the region. Women traveling solo or in small groups should expect some unwanted attention, particularly in more crowded urban areas. Dressing modestly (covered shoulders and knees) significantly reduces this. Traveling with a guide, especially for first-time visitors, provides additional comfort and a direct point of contact for any uncomfortable situations.

Many women travel Egypt solo every year without serious issues. It's worth going in with realistic expectations and the knowledge that having a local contact — like a tour guide — makes a real difference.

### What about Egypt's political situation?

Egypt has been politically stable since 2014. The tourism industry is important to Egypt's economy, and the government has invested heavily in security around tourist sites. Political demonstrations occur occasionally in central Cairo but are not a typical element of a tourist visit.

### Is food and water safe?

Drink bottled water — always. Don't use tap water for brushing teeth. Avoid ice in drinks from street vendors.

Food safety varies by establishment. Hotel restaurants and tour operator-arranged meals are generally reliable. Street food can be excellent — I eat it regularly — but for first-time visitors with sensitive stomachs, I recommend starting cautiously and expanding from there.

## How I Keep My Clients Safe

I've been guiding Americans through Egypt since 2002. Here's what actually makes a trip safe and comfortable:

**Proper logistics** — knowing which routes to take, which times of day to visit crowded sites (early morning is almost always better), and how to avoid situations that create problems.

**A reliable local contact** — having someone you can call if anything comes up makes an enormous difference to how confident you feel on the ground.

**Setting clear expectations** — Egypt is not Western Europe. It is loud, busy, and occasionally chaotic in the most entertaining way. Understanding that before you arrive means you're not unsettled by it when you get there.

**Vetted accommodations and transportation** — we use vehicles and hotels I've worked with for years. No surprises.
