# What to Pack for Egypt: A Practical List from Someone Who's Done This 22 Years

**By John Gamil** | BSc Egyptology | 22+ Years Guiding American Travelers Across Egypt | Founder, Access Egypt Travel
*Published: June 2026 | Last updated: June 2026*

---

The one thing I tell every American client before they travel: bring a scarf or lightweight shawl. Even in summer. You'll need it at mosques and Islamic sites, and it doubles as sun protection in the afternoon heat at Karnak. It weighs nothing. It solves two problems.

Most of what you actually need for Egypt fits in a carry-on. Here's the practical list — from someone who walks these sites multiple times a week.

---

## The Non-Negotiables

**Comfortable walking shoes** — Not sandals, not new shoes you're breaking in. Soft-soled, closed-toe shoes you've already walked miles in. You will cover 4–8 miles of uneven stone, sand, and ancient flooring on an active day. Blisters end trips early.

**Lightweight scarf or shawl** — Required to enter most mosques (shoulders and knees must be covered). Also useful for sun protection at open-air sites like Karnak in afternoon heat. A thin cotton scarf takes up no space.

**Sunscreen (SPF 50+)** — Egyptian sun is intense year-round. Reapply. Bring from home — pharmacy sunscreen in Egypt can be expensive and selection is limited.

**Sunglasses** — Non-negotiable. The combination of bright sun and highly reflective sand and limestone is genuinely punishing without them.

**Wide-brim hat** — A baseball cap works, but a wide brim hat protects your face, neck, and ears. Particularly important at open-air sites like the Valley of the Kings, where there's minimal shade.

**Refillable water bottle** — You'll go through significant water on site days. Your guide will provide bottled water, but having your own large bottle keeps you hydrated between stops. 1–1.5 liters is a reasonable target for mornings.

---

## Clothing: What to Bring

Egypt is a conservative culture in most contexts, though it's also a country accustomed to international visitors. Here's a practical approach:

**Lightweight, breathable fabrics** — Linen, cotton, moisture-wicking synthetics. Not denim. You'll be warm even in winter at some sites.

**Covered shoulders and knees** — Not required at archaeological sites, but required at mosques, most churches, and appreciated in markets and local neighborhoods. Packing 2–3 items that cover these areas gives you flexibility without extra luggage.

**Layers for winter travel (November–March)** — Egyptian evenings can be cool to cold, particularly in Cairo and Luxor in December–February. A light jacket or fleece is useful. Daytime can be warm; you'll shed layers by 10am.

**One nicer outfit** — If you're doing a cruise or dining at better restaurants in Cairo. Everything else can be practical.

**Loose-fitting pants or linen trousers** — More comfortable than shorts at many sites (particularly Muslim heritage sites), and cooler than denim in warm weather.

## Medications and Health

**Stomach medication and rehydration salts** — The most common Egypt health issue is digestive upset from new food and water. Bring Imodium, Pepto-Bismol, and a pack of oral rehydration salts. Hope you won't need them. Be glad they're there if you do.

**Antihistamines** — Dust, pollen, and sand can irritate eyes and sinuses, particularly during Khamsin wind season (spring). Useful to have.

**Prescription medications** — Bring more than you need, in original packaging, with a copy of your prescription. Egyptian pharmacies are generally well-stocked for common medications, but don't rely on it.

**Hand sanitizer** — Useful after crowded markets and before eating at outdoor spots.

---

## Electronics

**Plug adapter (Type C or F)** — Egypt uses European-style round two-pin plugs (220V). American electronics with flat prongs won't fit without an adapter. Buy before you leave — airport adapters are overpriced.

**Power bank** — Useful for long site days when you'll be away from charging points.

**Camera or charged phone** — You'll want photos. Download your photos nightly — don't risk a full card at Abu Simbel.

---

## What You Don't Need to Pack

**Egyptian pounds** — Exchange USD at the airport on arrival or use ATMs in Cairo, Luxor, or Hurghada. Don't buy EGP before you leave — rates outside Egypt are poor.

**A full pharmacy** — Egyptian pharmacies are modern, well-stocked, and affordable. Basic items are easy to find.

**Formal dress** — Egypt is not a formal country for tourists. Even nicer restaurants are casual.

**Heavy luggage** — Seriously. A checked bag plus a carry-on is more than enough for 10 days. You'll buy things in Egypt (papyrus, alabaster, spices, scarves) and need space on the way back.

---

## Packing by Season

**Summer (May–September):** Pack lightest — linen everything, minimal layers, maximum sun protection. You'll barely need anything after dark.

**Winter (December–February):** Add a fleece or light down jacket, one warmer sweater, and a pair of longer pants. Mornings and evenings can be cold, especially in Cairo.

**Spring/Fall (October–April):** The middle path — one light jacket, mix of warm and light layers.

---

## What to Buy in Egypt

Leave room in your luggage. The things worth bringing home:
- **Spices** from the spice market in Khan el-Khalili (cumin, coriander, za'atar blends)
- **Cotton textiles** — Egyptian cotton is genuinely excellent; towels and scarves make great gifts
- **Papyrus art** — buy from museum shops or reputable stores, not street vendors (most street papyrus is banana leaf)
- **Alabaster** — ornamental pieces from Luxor, particularly around the West Bank
