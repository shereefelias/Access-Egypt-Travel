# Best Time to Visit Egypt: Month-by-Month Guide for American Travelers

**By John Gamil** | BSc Egyptology | 22+ Years Guiding American Travelers Across Egypt | Founder, Access Egypt Travel
*Published: June 2026 | Last updated: June 2026*

---

October is my personal recommendation for first-timers. I've guided tours in every month of the year — October gives you the Valley of the Kings in the morning without the punishing heat that July brings, and the crowds are a fraction of the January peak. The light on sandstone monuments at 8am in October is also, for what it's worth, extraordinary.

But the best time to visit Egypt depends on what you're doing and what you're willing to deal with. Here's the honest breakdown.

---

## The Quick Answer

| Time | Conditions | Best For |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **October–November** | 25–32°C (77–90°F), low crowds | First-timers, all-around best |
| **February–April** | 20–28°C (68–82°F), peak season crowds | Comfortable weather, active atmosphere |
| **December–January** | 15–22°C (59–72°F), high crowds | Holidaymakers, cooler days |
| **May–September** | 38–45°C (100–113°F), low crowds | Budget travelers, heat-tolerant visitors |

## Month-by-Month Breakdown

### October & November — The Sweet Spot

Summer has broken. The heat is still warm — 28–32°C (82–90°F) in Luxor and Aswan — but nothing like the 42°C punish of July. Crowds are lighter than winter, prices begin to drop, and the Nile is at its greenest after the summer flood season.

This is when I'd send a first-time American visitor who has never been to Egypt and wants to see everything at a comfortable pace.

**Downside:** Almost none. If I'm being precise, early October can still be warm in Upper Egypt (Aswan, Abu Simbel). Later October and November are ideal.

### December & January — High Season

The most popular window, particularly for Americans on holiday schedules. Weather is genuinely pleasant — Cairo can be mild and breezy, Luxor and Aswan are cool and comfortable, the Red Sea resorts are warm enough to swim.

The tradeoff: you're sharing the sites with everyone else. The Valley of the Kings in January can feel like a crowded subway. Prices for flights and hotels are at their highest, and popular sites require early-morning visits to beat the crowds.

If December or January is when you can travel, it's still excellent. Just build early starts into your itinerary.

**Downside:** Crowds, prices, and the need to pre-book almost everything further in advance.

### February, March & April — Spring

One of the strongest windows to visit. Weather is excellent — warm days, cool evenings, low humidity. Crowds are lower than January, prices begin to soften, and spring light on the monuments is beautiful.

The Egyptian Museum and Karnak Temple are especially good in March — comfortable temperatures and manageable visitor numbers.

**Downside:** Khamsin winds can arrive in March–April — dust storms that roll in quickly, turn the sky amber, and reduce visibility. They're short-lived (usually hours), but worth knowing about.

### May — The Edge of Summer

Still manageable in the first half of May. The second half begins to feel hot in Upper Egypt. Red Sea resorts are still excellent for water activities. If you're primarily in Cairo, May is fine.

### June, July & August — Peak Heat

Upper Egypt (Luxor, Aswan, Abu Simbel) routinely hits 42–45°C (107–113°F) in July and August. This is extreme heat, and I'd be honest with you: it makes prolonged outdoor site visits physically demanding, even for people who consider themselves heat-tolerant.

I do guide tours in summer. Here's how: everything outdoors happens at sunrise. By 10am you're indoors, in air conditioning, or on the Nile. Afternoon is dead time. The reward: prices are at their lowest, crowds are at their thinnest, and you have major monuments nearly to yourself.

This is a genuine option for the right traveler — budget-conscious, early-riser, and interested in the surreal experience of standing alone in front of Abu Simbel at 6:30am.

**Downside:** It's very hot. I won't pretend otherwise.

### September — Transition

Still hot in Upper Egypt early in the month, beginning to moderate by late September. Red Sea remains excellent. Similar to the end of summer: fewer crowds, lower prices, heat manageable with early-morning scheduling.

## Best Time for Specific Activities

**Nile cruise (Luxor to Aswan):** October–April is ideal. Summer cruises are possible but very hot on deck.

**Red Sea diving and snorkeling:** Year-round, but April–May and September–October offer warm water and high visibility without summer crowds.

**Abu Simbel:** The February 22 and October 22 solar alignment events — when sunlight penetrates the temple to illuminate the inner sanctum statues — draw significant crowds. Book well in advance if targeting these dates.

**Cairo and the pyramids:** Year-round accessible. December–April is most comfortable. Summer mornings are manageable before 9am.

## What About Ramadan?

Ramadan is observed widely in Egypt, and the dates shift each year (based on the Islamic lunar calendar). During Ramadan, many restaurants have limited daytime hours, the atmosphere in cities is more religious and reflective, and some local operators adjust schedules.

Ramadan is not a reason to avoid Egypt — it's a fascinating time to observe Egyptian culture. But it does require more planning around meals and some site hours. Let us know if your travel dates overlap with Ramadan and we'll advise on what to expect.

## My Recommendation for First-Time American Visitors

**October or early November** if you want comfort, manageable crowds, and the best overall experience of the historic sites.

**February or March** if October doesn't work — nearly as good, with excellent weather and slightly lower prices than the January peak.
