# Cairo to Luxor: Your Complete Tour Guide (Routes, Sites, and What to Do)

**By John Gamil** | BSc Egyptology | 22+ Years Guiding American Travelers Across Egypt | Founder, Access Egypt Travel
*Published: June 2026 | Last updated: June 2026*

---

For most of my Cairo-based clients, I recommend the EgyptAir domestic flight over the long road transfer to Luxor. It's typically $60–90 USD and gets you there in 55 minutes — saving a full day of travel time you'd otherwise spend in a car watching desert. That day translates to one more temple, one more sunrise at the Valley of the Kings, one more evening on the Nile.

The Cairo to Luxor journey is one of the most significant transitions in Egypt travel — from the ancient capital of the north (Cairo and Giza) to the ancient capital of the south (Luxor, ancient Thebes). Two completely different experiences, two halves of ancient Egypt.

Here's how to do it well.

---

## Getting from Cairo to Luxor: Your Options

### Option 1: Domestic Flight (Recommended)

**EgyptAir** operates multiple daily flights between Cairo International Airport (CAI) and Luxor International Airport (LXG). Flight time is approximately 55 minutes.

**Cost:** $60–90 USD each way (book through egyptair.com; prices vary with booking window and season)

**Why I recommend this:** You're paying for time, not just a seat. The road journey takes 7–9 hours. That's a day of your trip. For most American visitors with 7–10 days in Egypt, that math is obvious.

Book domestic flights when you book your international flights, or at least 2–3 weeks in advance during busy seasons.

### Option 2: Overnight Sleeper Train

Egypt's overnight sleeper train (operated by Watania) runs between Cairo's Ramses Station and Luxor, departing in the evening and arriving the following morning. Journey time is approximately 10–11 hours.

**Cost:** Approximately $60–100 USD for a two-person sleeping compartment (you book by the compartment, so a solo traveler still pays for the full compartment)

**Who this is right for:** Travelers who enjoy the journey as part of the experience, or those who want to avoid a night's hotel cost while covering the distance. The train is comfortable — air-conditioned sleeping compartments with dinner service.

**Note:** The train is popular and books out well in advance during peak season (December–April). Don't try to book it the week before.

### Option 3: Private Vehicle (Not Recommended for This Route)

Yes, you can drive or take a private car from Cairo to Luxor. The distance is approximately 670 km (415 miles) — a 7–9 hour drive on the main route.

I don't recommend this for most travelers. It's a very long drive for a day tour distance, it ties up a full day in each direction, and the domestic flight achieves the same result in under an hour. The only time I'd suggest a private vehicle is if you specifically want to stop at sites along the way (such as Minya) and have extra days to dedicate.

## What to See: Cairo vs. Luxor

These two cities cover fundamentally different chapters of Egyptian history.

### Cairo + Giza: The New Kingdom's Northern Legacy

**Pyramids of Giza** — The Great Pyramid of Khufu, Khafre's pyramid with the Sphinx, and Menkaure's pyramid. These are 4,500 years old. Accessible from Cairo, best visited in the morning.

**Egyptian Museum (Cairo)** — The world's largest collection of ancient Egyptian artifacts, including the complete contents of Tutankhamun's tomb. Plan 2–3 hours minimum.

**Grand Egyptian Museum (Giza)** — The new museum adjacent to the pyramid plateau, opened in 2023. Houses artifacts that have never been on public display before, including artifacts from royal tombs. If you're visiting in 2025 onward, this is a must-see addition.

**Islamic Cairo** — Khan el-Khalili market, the old city, the Citadel of Saladin. A different Egypt from the ancient sites — Ottoman and Islamic architecture, living culture, excellent food.

### Luxor: Ancient Thebes, Capital of the New Kingdom

Luxor is divided by the Nile. The East Bank is for the living; the West Bank is for the dead. This was intentional — ancient Egyptians built their temples for the living gods on the sunrise (east) side, and their tombs in the direction of the setting sun (west).

**East Bank:**
- **Karnak Temple Complex** — The largest ancient religious site in the world. Built over 2,000 years by successive pharaohs. The Hypostyle Hall is one of the most awe-inspiring human-made spaces I've ever stood in.
- **Luxor Temple** — Connected to Karnak by the 2.7 km Avenue of Sphinxes, recently excavated and now walkable. Beautiful at sunset.

**West Bank:**
- **Valley of the Kings** — 63 royal tombs cut into the limestone cliffs. Entry includes 3 tombs. Tutankhamun's tomb requires a separate ticket (worth it).
- **Temple of Hatshepsut** — Three-tiered temple built into the cliff face by Egypt's most powerful female pharaoh.
- **Colossi of Memnon** — Two giant seated statues, all that remains of Amenhotep III's massive mortuary temple.
- **Medinet Habu** — Ramesses III's mortuary temple, less visited than the Valley of the Kings but extraordinarily well-preserved. One of my personal favorites to take clients to.

## How Many Days in Luxor?

**1 day (from Cairo):** Covers the main West Bank highlights (Valley of the Kings, Hatshepsut) if you arrive early.

**2 days:** West Bank day 1, East Bank day 2 (Karnak and Luxor Temple in golden hour). This is the minimum I recommend for Luxor to feel complete.

**3 days:** Adds Medinet Habu, a felucca ride on the Nile, more time at Karnak, and the option of a day trip to Esna Temple.

**3–4 days (Luxor + Aswan):** The full Upper Egypt experience. Adds Edfu, Kom Ombo, and Aswan — which includes Abu Simbel, Philae Temple, and the Nubian heritage sites.

**Day 5 — Karnak and Nile**
Karnak Temple (8am), Luxor Temple, felucca or motorboat on the Nile

**Day 6 — Fly back to Cairo or onward to Aswan**
