# Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum. Luxor holds one-third of all the monuments of the ancient world and 70% of Egypt's monuments. Founded as a capital of Egypt around 1500 BC, it became the most powerful city in the ancient world — home to the great pharaohs Tutankhamun, Ramesses II, Hatshepsut, Amenhotep III, and many more.

The city is divided by the Nile into two sides: the **East Bank** (Karnak Temples, Luxor Temple, Luxor Museum) and the **West Bank** (Valley of the Kings, Hatshepsut's Temple, Valley of the Queens, Deir el-Medina, Madinet Habu, Colossi of Memnon, and more).

While the Giza Pyramids may be Egypt's most famous landmark, many visitors say Luxor changes everything. Millions of tourists visit every year — and we always recommend spending as much time here as possible.

Luxor has an international airport (domestic and international flights), over 300 cruise ships operating between Luxor and Aswan, and train connections to Cairo and Aswan.

---

## Karnak Temple Complex

**The largest monumental complex on earth** — covering 200 acres with a history spanning 2,000 years. An extraordinary open-air museum containing temples, shrines, obelisks, columns, sphinxes, and much more.

**Location:** East Bank of Luxor.
**Hours:** Daily 6 a.m. – 5:30 p.m.
**Getting there:** 15–25 minutes by car.

---

## Luxor Temple

An iconic temple built approximately 1400 BC. Many pharaohs contributed to it — including Tutankhamun, Ramesses II, and even Alexander the Great. The temple is spectacular to visit in the evening when it is fully lit up — a completely different experience from the daytime visit. The famous **Sphinx Avenue** — a 2-mile processional road lined with ram-headed sphinxes — connects Luxor Temple to Karnak.

**Location:** City center, East Bank.
**Hours:** Daily 6 a.m. – 9 p.m.
**Getting there:** 10–15 minutes by car.

---

## Valley of the Kings

The ultimate dream of any traveler to Egypt. The burial place of the pharaohs of the New Kingdom period (since ~1500 BC), the Valley of the Kings offers the most vivid colors and extraordinary scenes of your entire trip. The ancient Egyptians kept this location secret to protect their royal mummies in the afterlife — tombs of Tutankhamun, Ramesses the Great, Seti I, and many more are here.

Each ticket includes 3 tombs; the extraordinary tomb of Ramesses V & VI is included as a highlight.

**Location:** West Bank of Luxor.
**Hours:** Daily 6 a.m. – 5 p.m.
**Getting there:** 30–45 minutes by car.

---

## Hatshepsut Temple (Deir el-Bahri)

**Built:** ~1478 BC

A breathtaking temple sunk into the heart of the limestone cliffs — partly carved into the mountain. A masterpiece of architecture commissioned by Hatshepsut, one of ancient Egypt's most powerful rulers, who reigned as Pharaoh for 22 years.

**Location:** West Bank of Luxor.
**Hours:** Daily 6 a.m. – 5 p.m.
**Getting there:** 30–45 minutes by car; only a few minutes from the Valley of the Kings.

---

## Colossi of Memnon

Two massive stone statues, each 60 feet high, flanking the entrance of the funerary temple of Amenhotep III (~1350 BC). The temple itself was the largest on the West Bank but was destroyed by an earthquake — only the two colossi remain, still rising majestically above the plain.

**Location:** West Bank of Luxor.
**Getting there:** A short stop on the way to the Valley of the Kings.

---

## Deir el-Medina (Valley of the Artisans)

**Since:** ~1520 BC

A hidden village in the heart of the Theban mountains — the home of the workers and artisans who dug and painted the royal tombs in the Valley of the Kings and Queens. One of the most important archaeological sites in Egypt for understanding ancient daily life, yet tragically overlooked by most tourists. The site includes workers' tombs, a small temple, and the remains of their houses.

**Location:** West Bank of Luxor.
**Hours:** Daily 6 a.m. – 5 p.m.
**Getting there:** 30–45 minutes by car; a few minutes from either the Valley of the Kings or Valley of the Queens.

---

## Valley of the Queens

The burial place for queens and other royal family members during the New Kingdom. Over 90 tombs, though most are unfinished. **Nefertari's Tomb** (wife of Ramesses II) — considered the most beautiful tomb in all of Egypt — is here, requiring a separate entrance ticket.

**Location:** West Bank of Luxor.

---

## Madinet Habu Temple

**Built:** ~1186–1155 BC (Ramesses III)

The magnificent memorial temple of Ramesses III — one of the least visited sites in Luxor, yet its colors and unique battle scenes are extraordinary. Visitors can also see the remains of Ramesses III's palace, which stood adjacent to the temple.

**Location:** West Bank of Luxor.
**Hours:** Daily 6 a.m. – 5 p.m.
**Getting there:** 30–45 minutes by car; a few minutes from the Valley of the Kings or Valley of the Queens.

---

## Dendera Temple

**Dedicated to:** Hathor (Goddess of Joy, love, music, and dancing)

One of the most important religious sites in ancient Egypt and one of the most preserved temples in the country, with vivid colors after restoration. Famous for the extraordinary astronomical ceiling depicting ancient zodiac signs and astrological scenes — and the so-called "light-bulb" carvings in an underground crypt. One of the least visited temples due to its distance from Luxor.

**Location:** About 50 miles (80 km) north of Luxor.
**Hours:** Daily 7 a.m. – 5 p.m.
**Getting there:** About 1.5 hours by car from Luxor.

---

## Hot Air Balloon over Luxor

The balloon flies over the West Bank of Luxor — a free pass over the most concentrated collection of temples and tombs in the ancient world. Pickup is very early in the morning; you cross to the West Bank by boat or car. The flight lasts about an hour. One of the most breathtaking excursions available anywhere in Egypt.

---

## Felucca Ride on the Nile

Relax on a traditional Egyptian sail-boat and watch the birds while gliding across the Nile with views of Luxor's skyline. The boat can take you to Banana Island on the far bank, where you can explore the farms, banana and citrus groves, crocodile pools, and local animals kept by the farmers.
